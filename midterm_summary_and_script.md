# ECON4200 Midterm Summary and Presentation Script

## Document Summary

This midterm presentation studies household expenditure and consumption patterns in Hong Kong from 1999 to 2019. The main research question is: what are the key patterns and trends in household expenditure and consumption over this period?

The project has three objectives:

1. Document trends in nominal and real household expenditure.
2. Compare expenditure composition across housing types.
3. Benchmark Hong Kong's consumption pattern against other Asian economies.

The presentation argues that household expenditure in Hong Kong increased over time, but much of the nominal rise was related to inflation. Even after adjusting for prices, however, housing still stands out as the most important category. At the aggregate level, housing remained the largest expenditure item, taking around 30% to 40% of total household spending, ahead of food and miscellaneous services. Housing also contributed the largest share of the increase in total expenditure over time.

When the analysis is broken down by housing type, the main pattern remains: housing is the dominant driver of household spending, especially for private and subsidized housing households. Private housing households spent about 38% to 44% of their budget on housing, and subsidized households spent about 32% to 40%, while public housing tenants faced a much lower housing share at about 12%. This suggests that the degree of housing subsidization strongly shapes observed expenditure patterns. Once housing is accounted for, the rest of the spending mix is relatively similar across groups.

The cross-country comparison reinforces this point. Hong Kong's housing expenditure share is much higher than in Singapore, South Korea, and Japan, which supports the view that housing occupies an unusually large place in the household budget compared with peer Asian economies. Overall, the findings are broadly consistent with the literature: food behaves like a standard necessity, households differ in spending patterns once we look below the aggregate level, and housing selection and affordability play a major role in shaping expenditure shares in Hong Kong.

## Script Part 1: Methodology (3 minutes)

**Slide 10**  
Let me explain our methodology for studying household expenditure and consumption patterns in Hong Kong from 1999 to 2019.

As suggested by earlier studies, household heterogeneity, necessity shares, and housing selection all matter, our methodology is designed to examine how these patterns exists and evolved in the Hong Kong data.

To do that, we combine both macro-level and micro-level data. At the aggregate level, we use national income and expenditure data together with consumer price information. These come from the World Bank and the Hong Kong CSD. At the household level, we use Hong Kong household expenditure data, from the Housing Expenditure Survey, lettting us break down spending into commodity groups such as housing, food, transport, durable goods, etc.

We also bring in comparative data for Singapore, Japan, and South Korea. This gives us a benchmark for interpreting Hong Kong's expenditure pattern in a broader regional context.

**Slide 11**
Our research is mainly descriptive. The goal is to document how household consumption evolved over time, how spending differs across housing types, and how Hong Kong compares with other Asian economies.

Methodologically, we do the followings.

First, we track expenditure relative to income over time at both the macro and household levels. This lets us see whether rising consumption follows income growth, or whether aggregate patterns hide important household differences.

Second, we examine the composition of household expenditure. Instead of only looking at the total amount spent, we break the household budget into major categories. Giving us a more clear view on the change of consumption patterns, and whether some categories, become more important over time.

Third, we control for inflation using the consumer price index. Allowing us to separate nominal price changes from real consumption growth. In other words, we ask whether households are truly consuming more, or they are simply paying higher prices for the goods.

Fourth, we compare households by housing type: public housing, subsidized housing, and private housing. This helps us see whether expenditure patterns vary with the degree of housing support, and whether the differences we observe are mainly linked to housing costs and housing selection.

Last but not least, we also benchmark to peer countries as another reference point. By comparing Hong Kong with Singapore, Japan, and South Korea, we can assess whether some of Hong Kong's expenditure patterns, are relatively unique, or broadly similar to those in nearby developed economies.

So in summary, our methodology descriptively analyse expenditure composition, with inflation adjustment and comparison across housing types, and benchmarking against other countries. Giving us a clear way to show not just that spending increased, but where that increase came from, and how Hong Kong's pattern can be interpreted in both a local and regional context.

## Script Part 2: Results on Consumption Pattern by Housing Type (3 minutes)

**Slide 18**
Lets focus on the results by housing type, where the household-level differences highlighted in the literature become clearest in our data.

The first key finding is that expenditure increased for all housing groups between 1999 and 2019, but not at the same pace. Public housing households saw the smallest increase, at about 17%. Subsidized housing households recorded a larger increase, at about 25%. Private housing households experienced the steepest increase, at about 39%. At the same time, the chart also shows that where housing support is weaker, households devote a higher share of income to consumption expenditure overall.

This pattern already tells us something important: the less housing support a household receives, the faster its expenditure rises. That is consistent with our findings of housing as the major component of consumption. The growth in total spending is not evenly spread across all categories. It is largely driven by housing spending.

**Slide 19**
The second key result comes from the expenditure shares. For private housing households, housing consistently takes the largest share of the budget, around 38% to 44%. For subsidized housing households, housing is also the largest category, around 32% to 40%. In contrast, for public housing households, the housing share stays much lower, at around 12%.

So the clearest distinction across housing types is not that households consume completely different baskets of goods. Rather, the major difference is how much of the household budget is absorbed by housing. Once that burden rises, it crowds out room for other spending. This is why housing is the dominant lens for interpreting consumption patterns in Hong Kong.

We also observe that the housing share continues to trend upward over time for private and subsidized households, by about 8 percentage points for private housing and about 6 percentage points for subsidized housing over the period shown. That means housing is not only the largest category, but also an increasingly important one. For public housing tenants, by contrast, food becomes relatively more prominent, with food's expenditure share rising by about 8 percentage points over time. This suggests that when housing costs are contained, households can allocate more of their budget toward other necessities.

Overall, consistent with the literature, higher-income households tend to devote a smaller share of their budget to food, while housing spending accounts for a larger and growing share of the household budget in Hong Kong, especially for private and subsidized households.

**Slide 20**  
When we adjust for inflation, most commodity groups show only modest real changes, so much of the earlier nominal increase is best read as coming from price movements rather than a broad rise in real consumption across the board. Housing is the key exception: for subsidized and private households, housing expenditure still rises in real terms, by about 6% and 8% respectively, even though public and subsidized households see weaker real consumption growth overall. That is why we emphasize that the housing pressure is not purely an inflation artifact.

This is an interesting pattern, because a pure price story would mainly explain nominal increases, not why housing also rises in real terms. This may be explained by the literature mentioned earlier, where households’ housing choices, including location, and quality preferences, can increase housing consumption, along with income increase, though our descriptive results cannot pin down the exact mechanism.

Therefore, our main interpretation is this: housing is the major driver of household consumption differences in Hong Kong. Private and subsidized households devote a very large and rising share of their spending to housing, while public housing tenants are relatively protected from that pressure. Once we control for housing, the rest of the consumption pattern looks much more similar across groups.

So taken together, these housing-type results support the broader story of the research. Nothing here contradicts the household expenditure evidence we already showed. The housing-type breakdown simply makes the same story sharper: housing clearly emerges as the main category shaping differences across groups.

So the takeaway from this section is simple: differences in consumption by housing type are, above all, differences in housing burden. In Hong Kong, housing is not just one spending category among many. It is the central force shaping the household budget.
