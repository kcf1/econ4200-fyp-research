### Possible questions / answers

| Question / criticism | How to answer (short) |

## Part 3 — Literature review

### Key points

- Chai et al. (2024): heterogeneity; budget shares **non-proportional** in income; richer households may **concentrate** spending—supports micro-level analysis.
- Anker (2011): Engel’s Law benchmark; food share falls with income; **standardize food** (e.g., exclude alcohol/tobacco) when comparing.
- Ho & Chiu (2002): accessibility/location and **sticky** housing spending; income shapes **housing selection**; public rental can **compress** housing burden vs private market.

### Possible questions / answers

| Question / criticism | How to answer (short) |
| --- | --- |
| Literature is thin / UK-focused / old Ho & Chiu piece. | We use three papers as **principles** (heterogeneity, Engel benchmark, HK housing selection), not as a full survey; a longer review is **future work**. |
| You cite Engel but then emphasize housing “overriding” Engel-type shifts. | Engel helps interpret **food**; HK’s **institutional housing market** can dominate the **budget share** even when food still behaves like a necessity in relative terms. |
| Representative agent vs heterogeneity: do you model heterogeneity formally? | No—we **document** group differences (housing tenure) and **compare distributions** descriptively; structural modeling is **beyond scope**. |

### Possible questions / answers

| Question / criticism | How to answer (short) |
| --- | --- |
| Script says “Housing Expenditure Survey” but materials also say general C&SD household expenditure—**which survey exactly**? | Use the **exact C&SD survey/module name** from your paper’s data appendix; if the script wording was loose, say **“household expenditure survey data from C&SD”** and cite the official publication. |

## Part 5 — Results: macro vs micro, levels, and composition

### Key points

- Macro: private consumption/GDP rises to ~**70%** by 2019; contrasts with EAP decline and flat world average.
- Micro nuance: average household expenditure rises after 2010, while **median income** diverges upward in later years → **median consumption-to-income** can look **less aggressive** than averages imply—composition matters.
- Levels: average monthly household expenditure ~**HK$19k (2005)** to ~**HK$30k (2019)**.
- Composition: housing **30–40%**, food ~**27%**, miscellaneous services ~**15%**; housing segment largest and growing in stacked bars.
- Decomposition of **+39%** total (1999–2019 narrative in script): housing contributes largest increment (**~22 ppt of 39**), food **~+11 ppt**, miscellaneous services; **composition shift**: housing share up ~**7 ppt**, food ~**+1 ppt**, other categories lose share → necessities-led reallocation, housing burden intensifies.

### Possible questions / answers

| Question / criticism | How to answer (short) |
| --- | --- |
| Average vs median: which should we trust? | **Median** for “typical” burden; **average** sensitive to top spenders; your deck already flags **divergence**—say you use both where available. |
| “Food share rose only 1% but contributed +11% to growth”—isn’t that confusing? | **Levels growth** can be large while **share** moves little if **total** rises; **share** answers “budget tilt,” **growth contribution** answers “where dollars went.” |
| Miscellaneous services is vague—what’s inside? | Answer with the **C&SD category definition** you used (subcomponents listed in your table notes). |

## Part 7 — Results by housing type

### Key points

- Nominal expenditure growth differs: public **~+17%**, subsidized **~+25%**, private **~+39%** (script/paper ballpark).
- Where subsidization is weaker, households devote a **higher share of income** to consumption expenditure overall—consistent with **housing-driven totals**.
- Budget shares: private housing housing **~38–44%**; subsidized **~32–40%**; public **~12%** housing share.
- Dynamics: private + subsidized housing shares **rise** (~**+8** and **+6** ppt over period in script); public tenants see **food share up ~+8 ppt** (housing contained → other necessities more salient).
- Real (script): subsidized/private still show **real housing spending up**; interpretation: not **purely** a price story—may reflect **quality/location/space** choices with income, but **mechanism not identified** descriptively.

### Possible questions / answers

| Question / criticism | How to answer (short) |
| --- | --- |
| Public housing households are **selected** (income/assets tests); composition differences may be **people**, not “housing support.” | Correct: we show **conditional patterns by tenure**; causal attribution needs **controls** (income, age, household size, district)—list as **extension**. |
| “Higher income households devote smaller food share” while public food rises—are you contradicting Engel? | Public-housing sample mixes **income levels** and **price/availability** constraints; interpret as **tenure-specific budget constraint**, not a universal Engel claim within that subgroup without controls. |
| “Crowding out” suggests optimization; do you test a formal budget model? | No—**language is economic intuition**; formally testing crowding out needs **prices, wages, savings**, and possibly **credit constraints**. |

## Part 8 — Cross-country comparison and “outlier” claims

### Key points

- HK housing share **~30–40%** of household spending vs much lower peers in your charts.
- Singapore: housing share **~18–24%**, upward but milder; more on education/health/financial services in narrative.
- Korea: housing share **~8–11%**, flatter; shift toward recreation/health/education.
- Japan: very stable overall spending; housing **<~8%** share; gradual recreation/health.
- Combined necessities (housing+food+transport): HK **>~60%** vs peers **<~50%** (script); optional reinforcement with **price-to-income** ratios (ensure **definitions and year** match the statistic you cite).

### Possible questions / answers

| Question / criticism | How to answer (short) |
| --- | --- |
| Japan’s low “housing expenditure share” often reflects **measurement** (imputed rent treatment, renter vs owner, cultural multi-generational housing). | We compare **official published household budget shares** consistently, but **interpret cautiously**; Japan may understate market rent burdens depending on survey rules. |
| Different **time windows** (Singapore 2002–2022, Korea 2009–2019, Japan 2004–2019) weaken comparison. | Acknowledge: exhibits use **available comparable years**; harmonizing **full overlap** is the robustness check to add. |
| Why not Taiwan / Mainland cities? | **Data harmonization and availability**; can be future comparator set if definitions align. |
| Price-to-income ratios (2025) vs expenditure shares (to 2019): mixing sources/years. | Use **either** as separate evidence; if shown together, label as **different indicators and years** so it is not read as one model. |

## Part 9 — Conclusion, policy, and future research

### Key points

- Nominal spending up, but **real** gains **modest**; **housing and durables** are main real growth areas; food **down** in real terms at aggregate level in your narrative.
- Tenure lens: **housing burden** is the primary **between-group** difference; **non-housing baskets** more similar once housing is accounted for (careful wording: “conditional patterns,” not “controlled” in a regression sense unless you ran controls).
- HK is a **regional outlier** in housing’s budget share and affordability indicators in your storyline.
- Next steps: drivers of **real housing consumption** (atomization, demographics, family structure); **macro shocks** transmission to households; **policy learning** from peer housing schemes—**descriptive** paper stops short of policy evaluation.

### Possible questions / answers

| Question / criticism | How to answer (short) |
| --- | --- |
| Policy recommendation is generic (“refine programs”). What **specific** lever? | We point to **peer schemes as case studies**; **cost–benefit and incidence analysis** is outside this midterm’s scope but is the right follow-on. |
| Could public housing expansion **distort** market signals / fiscal costs? | Reasonable debate; our paper **documents burdens** by tenure; full policy analysis needs **general equilibrium** and **fiscal incidence**. |
| What about **wealth/income inequality** driving averages? | Important: inequality can **separate mean vs median** paths; extending to **percentiles** is a natural robustness extension. |
